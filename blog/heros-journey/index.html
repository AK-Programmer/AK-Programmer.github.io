A Hero's Journey

August 21, 2020

Lately, I’ve been thinking about what I want to do with my life, as one does. Thus far, the answer is, “I’m not entirely sure”.

There are a bunch of things I really enjoy doing, but right now it’s hard to definitively say what I’d like to dedicate the rest of my life to, if anything. Yet, I still have a general, vaguely defined sense of where I’d like to end up. So, in an attempt to better define this desire, I sought useful analogiesâ€“ones that would help me communicate it to others and to provide me with some more direction.

Treating your life like a video game where you have to level up your character, go on missions and defeat final bosses is a surprisingly fulfilling perspective

As someone who played a lot of video games when I was younger, I remember spending hours tirelessly acquiring resources, defeating enemies, and becoming more powerful. I also remember almost always feeling a little guilty after having made significant progress in some game, as that “progress” was obviously meaningless.

Spinning my old pastime into a pseudo-philosophy by which I could live my life was motivating, but I still felt that it was missing something. After some time, I recalled an archaic archetype that has permeated stories and mythology for millenia: the hero’s journey.

The hero’s journey archetype can be roughly broken down into the following steps [1]:

  1. An ordinary world: You are living your normal life, and are perhaps dissasisfied with some aspect of it, be it personal failures, problems with your relationships, or just the blandness of it all.

  2. A call to adventure: You are confronted with a challenge or opportunity that is all too appealing. To pursue it is to change the trajectory of your life.

  3. Meeting the mentor: You encounter someone or something that prepares you for the perilous events ahead of you, and imbues upon you a profound insight (or several).

  4. A series of challenges: Now you actually begin your journey, facing numerous hardships thereafter, and working your way towards your goal.

  5. The ordeal: Then, you face the biggest, deadliest challenge of all. One that could very well spell your destruction. By sheer determination, skill, and luck, you not only survive, but triumph.

  6. The return: After unimagineable hardship, you finally achieve your goal. You return home a changed person.

In short, it describes a process of discovery, adversity, triumph, and transformation. So, what journeys can aspiring heros embark on in the twenty-first century? Here are a few ideas off the top of my head:

  • Running a marathon
  • Starting a company. Struggling to prevent your company from failing.
  • Going on a literal adventure (summitting Mount Everest, hunting, serving in the army)
  • Growing emotionally
  • Overcoming trauma
  • Quitting bad habits and addictions
  • Overcoming an insecurity
  • Earning a rigorous academic degree
  • Learning a new skill

As you might have noticed, most of these goals aren’t necessarily characterized by a hero’s journey arc. However, my guess is that characterizing them as such can help frame otherwise difficult times during whatever ‘journey’ you happen to be on in a positive light and push you to seek new experiences. y

Even for the most adventurous of us, life usually ends up mostly being a daily slog, with intermittent moments of excitement, failure, and gratification. I’m not expecting this metaphor to completely change my trajectory, but so long as it positively changes my perspective, it will have done its job.

Notes

* One of the most impactful pieces of advice I’ve heard is “do it for the story.” I like to think of the ‘hero’s journey’ as an extension of that.

[1] The breakdown described here isn’t one you might find elsewhere. I simply included the most relevant elements.
